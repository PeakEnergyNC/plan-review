Section 3

Priority Issue: Second-Floor HVAC in Vented Attic

⚠ HIGHEST IMPACT ITEM ON THIS PROJECT

Based on the plans, the second-floor air handler, equipment, and all associated ductwork are located in the vented attic above the second-floor ceiling. In Climate Zone 3A, this is the single largest driver of comfort problems, high energy bills, and humidity issues in new construction.

In this climate zone, attic temperatures reach 130–150°F in summer. Duct systems in that environment lose 15–25% of heating and cooling capacity before conditioned air reaches occupied space. The effect is not marginal — it is the reason homeowners with otherwise well-built homes report rooms that will not cool down in August, humidity problems on upper floors, and HVAC systems that run continuously.

DOE Efficient New Homes V2 Rev.3 (issued 9/15/2025; updated 3/16/2026) requires all heating and cooling ducts and air handlers to be located within the thermal and air barrier boundary. With the second-floor system in the vented attic, this home does not currently meet that benchmark. Addressing this is strongly recommended for comfort, efficiency, and long-term performance regardless of whether certification is pursued.

Options to Move the Second-Floor System Inside the Thermal Boundary

Option A — Conditioned attic (spray foam at roof deck)

Apply closed-cell spray foam to the underside of the roof sheathing and gable walls to achieve approximately R-30. Closed-cell delivers R-6 to R-7 per inch — R-30 requires approximately 4.5–5 inches. Open-cell spray foam (R-3.5/inch) would require approximately 9–10 inches for the same R-value and is not recommended for this roof deck application. This approach converts the attic from vented to conditioned space, enclosing the existing HVAC equipment and ductwork within the thermal boundary without relocating them — often the lowest-disruption path when the system is already roughed in. The ridge/soffit vents are eliminated.

Spray foam quality note: Spray foam is highly sensitive to installation quality. Incorrect mixing ratios, substrate temperature below manufacturer specification, or insufficient application thickness produce foam that fails to cure properly — with actual R-values far below the nominal rating. Require a contractor with documented residential roof deck experience and commercial-grade equipment. Conduct an on-site quality review during the application day. Verify target thickness using depth-check pins before the foam is covered. Do not accept visual inspection of foam color or texture as the sole quality indicator.

Option B — Interior duct chase from second floor

Frame interior duct chases on the second floor and route ductwork through conditioned space through interior soffits or dropped ceilings. The air handler remains in the attic in a partially encapsulated mechanical space. More complex to execute after framing, but achievable.

Option C — Relocate air handler to conditioned second-floor closet

Move the second-floor ducted air handler into a conditioned mechanical closet within the second-floor footprint with short duct runs entirely in conditioned space. Requires coordination with floor plan and structural openings. Cleanest long-term solution.

L1-D   Airtight Recessed Lighting

Any recessed can lights installed in ceilings below the unconditioned attic must be IC-rated and airtight-rated (ICAT). Recessed cans that are not airtight create direct air pathways between conditioned space and the attic and are among the most common air leakage failures at blower door testing.

L1-E   Radiant Barrier in Vented Attic

Vented attic only. A radiant barrier is appropriate only for vented attic assemblies. Do not install a radiant barrier if Option A (conditioned attic via closed-cell spray foam at the roof deck) is selected — the insulated roof deck assembly replaces the function of a radiant barrier, and applying one in a conditioned attic provides no meaningful benefit. A radiant barrier is not appropriate for spray foam or unvented attic assemblies.

Because the second-floor HVAC and ductwork are currently in the vented attic, a radiant barrier on the underside of the roof sheathing will reduce attic temperatures and improve equipment performance until the duct location issue is resolved. DOE research shows radiant barriers can reduce cooling energy by 5–10% in homes with ductwork in vented attics in the Southeast.

If the vented attic configuration is retained, install the radiant barrier during framing before sheathing is placed, or as a foil-faced product applied to the underside of the sheathing at the same time as the duct installation. If the attic is later converted to conditioned space under Option A, the radiant barrier is not needed and should not be installed over the spray foam.

☑ HERO ☑ ENERGY STAR

L2-A   Advanced Framing Techniques (Optimum Value Engineering)

Structural drawings show 2×6 studs at 16″ O.C. on both floors. Advanced framing (also called Optimum Value Engineering or OVE framing) reduces thermal bridging at corners, headers, and partition intersections — locations where studs interrupt the insulation plane.

Program note: Advanced framing techniques are no longer required under ENERGY STAR Section 3 as a mandatory checklist item. However, ENERGY STAR continues to highly recommend them as a best practice for thermal performance, and the ENERGY STAR Thermal Bypass Inspection Checklist still requires that identified bridging locations be properly addressed. ANSI/RESNET/ICC 301 establishes insulation installation quality grades (Grade I, II, III) — advanced framing directly supports Grade I installation by ensuring full cavity fill with no voids at corners, headers, and intersections. All references in this section reflect ENERGY STAR recommendations and ICC 301 best practice, not mandatory requirements.

What this means for this project:

  • Studs at 24″ O.C. instead of 16″ O.C. (primary recommendation) — Reduces the framing factor from approximately 25% to approximately 15%, increasing the insulated wall area. Uses 5–10% less lumber by board-feet. Fully compatible with 2×6 framing at this wall height. Confirm with structural engineer against load requirements for this project.
  • Two-stud corners with drywall clips — Standard framing uses three or four studs at corners, creating solid wood where insulation should go. Two-stud corners (California corners) with drywall clips provide the same structural performance with continuous insulation in the corner cavity.
  • Insulated headers — Headers over windows and doors in exterior walls are typically solid dimensional lumber, which is fully thermally bridged. In 2×6 walls with lower structural loads, right-sized headers with rigid foam insulation on the interior face maintain structural performance while eliminating the solid-wood thermal bridge. For this project, cut ½-inch rigid foam board sections and insert them into the header cavity alongside batt insulation before drywall is installed.
  • Single top plates (where code and loads permit) — Reduces the amount of solid wood at the top of the wall, where top plate bridging occurs across multiple stud bays.
  • Ladder blocking at partition wall intersections — Interior partition walls meeting exterior walls typically use a dedicated stud for drywall nailer. Ladder blocking achieves the same drywall backing with significantly less solid wood, eliminating a thermal bridge location.

The ENERGY STAR Thermal Bypass Inspection Checklist, completed at pre-drywall by a qualified third-party inspector, requires that identified bridging locations be properly addressed. Advanced framing techniques are the most effective way to resolve corner, header, and partition intersection items on that checklist. Even where studs remain at 16″ O.C., insulated headers and two-stud corners eliminate the highest-impact thermal bridges and directly support Grade I insulation installation per ANSI/RESNET/ICC 301.

If 16″ O.C. framing is already set based on structural requirements or builder preference, the insulated header and two-stud corner techniques still apply at those specific locations without changing the overall stud spacing.

L2-B   Wall Insulation — R-21 High-Density Fill

Plans confirm 2×6 framing on both floors. NC Code Minimum for CZ 3A: R-15 cavity, or R-13 + R-2.5 continuous insulation (ci), per Table N1102.1.2 (R402.1.2), 2018 NC Energy Conservation Code. HERO and ENERGY STAR target R-21 high-density batts or dense-pack cellulose — both achieve full cavity fill with better thermal performance than standard R-19 at the same framing depth.

Rim and band joist (full house):

Apply open-cell spray foam to the full length of the rim/band joist on both floors — including the garage-adjacent rim joists. Open-cell foam fully encapsulates the joist bay, seals all penetrations through the sill plate and band, and handles the irregular geometry at the floor perimeter better than cut-and-cobble rigid foam. Minimum 3–4 inches of open-cell to achieve R-12 or better at the rim. This is one of the highest air-leakage locations in the building envelope and is critical for both HERO and ENERGY STAR Thermal Bypass compliance.

Exterior walls behind tubs and showers:

Any exterior wall cavity behind a tub or shower unit must be insulated and air sealed before the unit is installed. Once the tub or shower pan is set, this cavity is inaccessible. Install R-21 batt or open-cell spray foam in these cavities and seal all penetrations before the plumbing fixtures are positioned. This is an ENERGY STAR Thermal Bypass checklist item and a common inspection failure.

L2-C   Attic Insulation — R-49 Blown Cellulose or Fiberglass

NC Code allows R-38 for CZ 3A. ENERGY STAR thermal envelope performance is verified against the ENERGY STAR Reference Home Design — a reference model built to 2021 IECC prescriptive values — rather than requiring specific prescriptive R-values. DOE Efficient New Homes independently requires that the rated home's total thermal envelope UA meet or fall below the 2021 IECC prescriptive UA. In practice, R-49 blown cellulose or fiberglass on the attic floor is the most common and straightforward way to meet both programs' Reference Home UA thresholds for CZ3A. With stick-framed 2×8 rafters at 16″ O.C., this home does not have raised-heel (energy) trusses that would allow full insulation depth to extend over the exterior wall top plates at the eaves.

At minimum, install R-49 blown cellulose or fiberglass in the field of the attic. At the eave areas where rafter depth limits insulation thickness, use a high-density insulation dam board or rigid foam overlay. The attic access door area must be addressed separately per L1-B.

Raised-heel trusses or rafter heels:

This roof should be framed with raised-heel trusses (also called energy trusses). The current plan shows stick-framed 2×8 common rafters at 16″ O.C., which limits insulation depth at the eave to roughly 3–4 inches — effectively R-10 or less directly above the exterior wall, the highest heat-loss location in the attic floor. Raised-heel trusses add height at the outside wall top plate, allowing full-depth insulation to extend uninterrupted from the center of the attic all the way to the eave. Specify a minimum 10–11 inch clear heel height to allow full R-38 to R-49 depth at the exterior wall line. The structural engineer needs to spec and approve the raised-heel truss design, including heel height, bearing details, and any lateral bracing changes.

This applies regardless of insulation method. If Option A (conditioned attic via spray foam at roof deck) is selected for the duct location issue, the roof deck becomes the insulation plane — but spray foam still needs full depth at the eave to maintain a continuous thermal boundary. Without a raised heel, there is not enough clearance at the eave to achieve 10–11 inches of spray foam where the roof deck meets the top plate. A raised heel provides the space needed for both blown insulation on the attic floor (standard vented attic) and spray foam at the roof deck (conditioned attic).

Soffit ventilation baffles (vented attic):

If the attic remains vented, proper ventilation baffles (also called rafter vents or insulation baffles) must be installed at every soffit vent bay before insulation is blown. The baffles maintain a clear air channel from the soffit to the ridge, but they must be positioned so that full-depth insulation can still extend all the way to the top of the exterior wall. If the baffles are installed too low or too flat against the roof sheathing, they push the insulation away from the eave and leave the top plate area underinsulated — defeating the purpose of the raised heel. Install baffles tight to the underside of the roof sheathing, running from the soffit opening up past the top plate, with the bottom edge of the baffle sitting at or above the top of the exterior wall so blown insulation fills completely beneath it to full depth.

L2-D   Crawl Space Encapsulation

Plans show a crawl space foundation with CMU masonry walls and CMU piers. Builder confirms the first-floor HVAC and ductwork are in the sealed crawl space, which requires the crawl to function as conditioned or semi-conditioned space. HERO requires a fully encapsulated crawl space.

For a sealed crawl space with HVAC inside, the minimum assembly is:

  • Foundation vent openings sealed (no passive vents to exterior)
  • 12–20 mil reinforced vapor barrier on exposed ground, lapped up foundation walls minimum 6 inches and taped at seams
  • Vapor barrier mechanically fastened and taped to foundation walls
  • CMU piers sealed at base with vapor barrier lapping up sides
  • Rim joist insulation and air sealing (closed-cell foam or rigid foam with foam sealant)
  • Conditioned air supply or stand-alone dehumidifier to manage humidity in the crawl space envelope

Summer humidity in this area is 70–90% RH from May through September. A vented crawl in this climate draws in more moisture than it removes.

Foundation wall insulation:

For a sealed crawl space, insulating the CMU foundation walls reduces heat transfer and keeps the crawl environment closer to indoor conditions year-round. Recommended approach:

  • Primary recommendation: R-10 foil-faced rigid polyisocyanurate or XPS board mechanically fastened to the interior face of the CMU walls, seams taped with foil tape. Foil-faced board provides a clean, durable interior finish and is easier for inspectors and future service personnel to assess. Bring the board down to the vapor barrier and seal at the base.
  • Alternative: 2–3 inches of closed-cell spray foam applied directly to the CMU wall face. Spray foam conforms to the masonry surface and bonds directly — no fasteners required. More expensive per R-value than rigid board but provides a seamless air and vapor barrier in a single application.

Either approach is acceptable. The foil-faced rigid board option is generally preferred for CMU crawl walls because it is neater, easier to inspect, and produces a finished appearance. Confirm that the selected product meets any local fire-rating requirements for exposed foam insulation in the crawl space.

L3-D   Exterior Continuous Insulation — R-5 Rigid Foam Board (Optional Upgrade)

The 2018 NC Energy Conservation Code (Table N1102.1.2) recognizes exterior continuous insulation (ci) as an alternative or supplement to cavity insulation. For 2×6 walls in Climate Zone 3A, the code-minimum approach with continuous insulation is R-13 cavity + R-2.5 ci. Adding exterior rigid foam board improves the effective whole-wall R-value by interrupting thermal bridging at studs, which account for roughly 15–25% of total wall area in standard 16″ O.C. framing. Cavity insulation alone cannot address this bridging — the foam layer must be continuous across the face of the framing.

One inch of polyisocyanurate (approximately R-6/inch) or XPS (R-5/inch) rigid foam board applied to the exterior face of the wall sheathing, beneath the cladding, delivers R-5 continuous insulation. Combined with R-21 cavity fill in 2×6 framing, this produces a meaningfully higher effective whole-wall R-value than cavity fill alone. The improvement is most significant at corners, window/door jambs, and stud-dense framing areas.

For this project:

  • Apply 1-inch polyiso or XPS continuous foam board over the wall sheathing, full building perimeter
  • Tape all seams with manufacturer-recommended compatible tape — the taped foam layer can serve as or back the weather-resistive barrier depending on the product specified
  • Extend window and door bucks (jamb extensions) to account for the added wall thickness
  • Coordinate cladding attachment details with the framing contractor — the cladding fasteners must penetrate through the foam into the studs; use screws of appropriate length per manufacturer guidance
  • Where foam board is exposed above-grade, protect from UV degradation and pest intrusion with pressure-treated trim, metal flashing, or cladding lapped to grade

This is an optional upgrade, not a code requirement for this project. It is most cost-effective when the decision is made before sheathing is complete — adding continuous insulation after the fact requires new cladding and extended bucks throughout. At the framing stage, the incremental material cost is modest relative to the whole-wall thermal improvement.

Garage Planning Notes

Plans show a 999 SF conditioned garage with a vehicle lift. Builder has asked specific questions about the garage thermal envelope. Recommendations below address those questions directly.

Garage Slab — Perimeter Insulation

Structural drawings show a 4-inch poured concrete slab on grade with 6-mil polyethylene vapor barrier. For a conditioned garage slab, upgrade to 10-mil reinforced vapor barrier sealed at all seams — 10-mil is the appropriate standard for a heated or conditioned slab environment and provides better puncture resistance during construction.

Code requirement — heated vs. unheated slab: Under 2018 NCECC Table R402.1.2, slab perimeter insulation requirements depend on climate zone, slab type, and whether the slab is heated. In Climate Zone 3A, an unheated slab on grade does not trigger a perimeter insulation requirement. A heated slab (e.g., in-slab radiant) does require perimeter insulation per the same table. Regardless of code mandate, R-10 extruded polystyrene at the garage slab perimeter is strongly recommended for thermal comfort and performance, and may support above-code program targets for conditioned garage floors.

Recommended installation regardless of code mandate: R-10 extruded polystyrene (XPS) or closed-cell polyisocyanurate at the slab perimeter, extending from the top of the slab down to a minimum 2-foot depth. Protect the above-grade portion from UV degradation and pest entry with pressure-treated boards, stucco parging, or metal flashing.

2018 NC Energy Conservation Code (NCECC)

Garage Exterior Walls and Ceiling

For a fully conditioned garage, the exterior walls and ceiling should be insulated to the same standard as the house:

  • Exterior walls: R-21 high-density fill in 2×6 cavities with continuous air sealing at all penetrations
  • Ceiling / roof assembly: R-49 blown insulation on the attic floor above, or spray foam at the roof deck if no attic above
  • Garage door: specify an insulated door with a minimum R-12 polyurethane foam core — uninsulated steel doors are a significant thermal weak point in a conditioned garage

Floor Assembly Above Garage

Where occupied living space sits directly above the garage, the floor/ceiling assembly must be fully insulated with no air gap between the insulation and the floor deck above:

  • Blown fiberglass or cellulose to full joist cavity depth, installed with netting stapled to the bottom of the floor joists — ensures insulation fills completely against the subfloor with no convective air gap
  • Alternatively: open-cell spray foam applied from below to fill the entire joist cavity — achieves continuous contact with the floor deck and provides combined air sealing and insulation in one step
  • An air gap between the top of the insulation and the floor deck significantly reduces effective R-value through convective bypass — this is one of the most common floor insulation installation deficiencies

Air sealing is as important as insulation at this assembly. Seal all penetrations through the floor — electrical boxes, pipes, duct boots — with canned foam before insulation is placed. This prevents garage air (vehicle exhaust, stored chemicals) from migrating into living space.

House-to-Garage Separation Wall

Per 2018 NC Residential Code Table R302.6, the garage must be separated from the residence and its attic by not less than ½-inch gypsum board applied to the garage side of the common wall. Where habitable rooms occur above the garage, the garage ceiling must be protected by not less than ⅝-inch Type X gypsum board. For thermal performance, insulate the house-to-garage separation wall with R-13 or R-15 batts in 2×4 cavities, or R-21 in 2×6 cavities. Air sealing at this assembly is equally critical: seal all penetrations — electrical boxes, pipes, ducts — with canned foam before drywall is installed. This prevents garage air, including vehicle exhaust and stored chemicals, from migrating into conditioned living space through gaps in the assembly.

Future Mini-Split for Garage

Builder plans to add a mini-split to the garage in the future. During rough-in, install:

  • 240V/30A dedicated circuit at the planned head unit location
  • Line set penetration sleeve through the exterior wall or ceiling (insulated and sealed)
  • Condensate drain line rough-in at the head unit location

ENERGY STAR Checklists Overview

ENERGY STAR Certified Homes v3.2 includes two field-verified checklists, completed by a third-party verifier during construction. These checklists are valuable as a construction quality framework regardless of whether formal ENERGY STAR certification is pursued — they identify the specific field conditions that most commonly lead to performance problems, callbacks, and comfort complaints in newly built homes. Using them as a reference costs nothing and catches issues at the stage when they are cheapest to correct.

Thermal Bypass Inspection Checklist

The ENERGY STAR Thermal Bypass Inspection Checklist identifies specific locations where air movement and thermal bridging can bypass the intended insulation and air barrier system. It is performed at the pre-drywall stage by a qualified third-party inspector. Note: ENERGY STAR Section 3 no longer requires advanced framing as a mandatory item — advanced framing is now a highly recommended best practice that directly addresses multiple checklist locations. ANSI/RESNET/ICC 301 insulation installation Grade I is the quality standard underlying these requirements. Key checklist items relevant to this project:

  • Attic kneewalls (if present): rigid air barrier on attic side, insulation on attic side
  • Attic hatches and access doors: insulated and gasketed to full attic R-value
  • Dropped ceilings and soffits: rigid air barrier installed on top before insulation is placed
  • Exterior walls: air barrier at all penetrations, no gaps at window/door rough openings, rim/band joist sealed and insulated
  • Cantilevered floors: insulation immediately below floor sheathing, air barrier on all exposed sides

Appendix C

Advanced Framing — Reference Resources

Advanced framing (Optimum Value Engineering, or OVE) reduces structural lumber in exterior walls while maintaining structural performance. The result is less thermal bridging, more insulated wall area, and in most cases a modest reduction in lumber cost. Techniques are well-established and documented by multiple research programs.

ENERGY STAR no longer mandates advanced framing under Section 3 of its Certified Homes requirements. Advanced framing is strongly recommended by ENERGY STAR, supported by ANSI/RESNET/ICC 301 (insulation installation quality grades), and is the most effective method for achieving Grade I insulation installation at corners, headers, and partition intersections.
